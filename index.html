Nominative The (noun)

The nominative is the subject of the sentence, the noun doing the action. For example, puella currit means "the girl runs." Puella is nominative because she is the one doing the running.

Genitive Of the (noun)

The genitive shows possession or belonging. It is translated with "of the" or with an apostrophe-s. For example, aqua villae means "the water of the house," and puella villae means "the girl of the house."

Dative To / for the (noun)

The dative shows who receives something or who benefits from an action. It is translated with "to" or "for." For example, aquam villae dat means "he gives water to the house," and vītam puellae dat means "she gives life for the girl."

Accusative The (noun) — direct object

The accusative is the direct object, the noun receiving the action of the verb. For example, puellam videt means "he sees the girl," where puellam is the one being seen. It is also used after prepositions showing movement toward something, such as ad villam, "to the house."

Ablative From · With · By · In · On

The ablative is the most flexible case and covers a range of meanings. It can mean "from" as in ex villā, "out of the house." It can mean "with" as in cum puellā, "with the girl." It can mean "by" as in aquā, "by means of water." And it can mean "in" or "on" as in in villā, "in the house." The meaning depends on context and whether a preposition is used.
